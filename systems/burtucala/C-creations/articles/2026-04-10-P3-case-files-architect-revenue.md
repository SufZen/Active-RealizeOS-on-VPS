# Why Your Architect Designs Beautiful Spaces That Lose Money

**Category:** Real Estate / Entrepreneurship | **Pillar:** Space, Design & Brand

## Starting Point

An investor approached us with a 220sqm property in a Lisbon neighborhood with strong tourism traffic. The concept: a 6-room boutique guesthouse with a small cafe on the ground floor. Budget ceiling: 180,000 for renovation.

The architect had already produced floor plans. The spaces were elegant. Natural light was maximized. The common area was generous and inviting. The cafe had a beautiful open layout with a long marble counter.

On paper, it looked right. In the revenue model, it lost 2,400 per month.

## Diagnostic

When we ran the numbers, 3 problems emerged that the architectural plans could not show:

**Problem 1: The common area was 35sqm.** That is 16% of total space dedicated to a zone that generates zero direct revenue. In a 220sqm property, every square meter matters. A common area of 15 to 20sqm would have been sufficient for 6 rooms while freeing 15 to 20sqm for a 7th room.

One additional room at an average nightly rate of 85 and 60% occupancy produces approximately 18,600 in annual revenue. That is the cost of a generous common area: not what you spend building it, but what you lose by not using the space productively.

**Problem 2: The cafe layout seated 18 but the kitchen supported 12.** The architect designed the dining space for 18 covers but sized the kitchen at 8sqm. With Portuguese **HACCP** (food safety compliance) requirements, 8sqm limits you to prep-only: no serious cooking, no hot food service. The actual kitchen capacity supported 12 covers of light food, not the 18 the seating suggested.

The gap between seating capacity and kitchen capacity meant the cafe would either under-serve (empty seats during busy periods) or need kitchen expansion (additional 15,000 to 25,000 in renovation costs plus permit delays).

**Problem 3: The storage was designed for aesthetics, not operations.** Beautiful built-in closets in every room. But no housekeeping closet on either floor. No linen storage. No maintenance supplies area. The operational team would need to carry supplies from the basement (which was not in the architectural scope) to every room, every day.

This is not an error. It is a difference in what architects optimize for. Architects design spaces for experience and beauty. Ventures need spaces designed for revenue per sqm and operational efficiency.

## Decision

We recommended 3 changes to the floor plan:

1. **Reduce the common area from 35sqm to 18sqm.** Use the freed space for a 7th room. Net revenue impact: +18,600 per year.

2. **Resize the kitchen from 8sqm to 14sqm.** This brought actual cooking capacity to match the 18-seat cafe. It required moving one internal wall and adjusting the HACCP plan. Cost: approximately 4,000. Revenue impact: the cafe could now serve hot food, increasing average ticket from 6 to 12.

3. **Add a 6sqm housekeeping and storage zone on the first floor.** Taken from the oversized hallway. Zero impact on room sizes. Operational impact: housekeeping time per room reduced by an estimated 15 minutes per day (no basement trips).

The architect was not asked to redesign the property. The changes were specific, data-driven adjustments to a plan that was already architecturally sound.

## Outcome

After adjustments, the revenue model shifted from a 2,400 monthly loss to a projected 1,800 monthly surplus at 60% occupancy. The renovation budget stayed within the 180,000 ceiling. Total additional costs from the changes: approximately 4,000.

The architectural plans remained beautiful. They just now supported a business that could sustain itself.

## Lessons

- **Architects optimize for space quality. Ventures need space productivity.** These are not the same thing. Both matter. But if revenue per sqm is not part of the brief, the design will default to aesthetics.

- **3 questions to ask your architect before approving plans:**
  1. What is the revenue-generating sqm versus non-revenue sqm ratio?
  2. Does the kitchen/service capacity match the customer-facing capacity?
  3. Where does the operational team store supplies, clean, and manage the space?

- **The Burtucala Space Program.** We create a space program before the architect starts drawing. It defines zones, sqm allocations, capacity per zone, and operational flow. The architect receives a brief that includes business logic, not just spatial requirements.
