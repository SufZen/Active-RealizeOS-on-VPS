# The 3 Emails That Actually Get Portuguese Bureaucrats to Respond

**Category:** Entrepreneurship | **Pillar:** Energy & Efficiency

Over the past 18 months, I have sent approximately 47 emails to Portuguese government offices. **Camara Municipal** (city hall), **Finanças** (tax authority), and various departmental contacts. Of those 47, only 12 received responses. And every response came from one of 3 email formats.

The rest? Silence. Not because the officials are unhelpful. Because the format was wrong.

## What Went Wrong

My first emails were written the way I write to business contacts: direct, concise, with a clear ask. Subject line: "Question about permit application." Body: 2 sentences and a question.

In most business contexts, that works. In Portuguese government communication, it does not.

Portuguese institutional communication follows a formal protocol. Emails that skip the protocol get deprioritized. Not intentionally. They just do not match the expected pattern, and in a queue of hundreds of requests, the familiar format gets processed first.

## The 3 Formats That Work

### Format 1: The Formal Request (Requerimento Style)

This mirrors the structure of a **requerimento** (formal written request) that is used for official Portuguese government submissions. Even in email, the structure signals seriousness.

**Structure:**
- **Subject line:** Include your NIF or process number. "Ref: Processo n.o [number] / NIF [number]"
- **Opening:** "Exmo(a) Senhor(a)," (Esteemed Sir/Madam). Always formal.
- **Identification paragraph:** State your full name, NIF, and the specific process or application you are referencing.
- **Request paragraph:** State exactly what you need. One specific request per email. Not 3 questions bundled together.
- **Closing:** "Agradeço a atenção dispensada." (Thank you for your attention.) Then your full name and contact.

This format works because it matches what government staff expect. It can be processed, filed, and forwarded within their system.

### Format 2: The Follow-Up With Protocol Number

When following up on a submitted application, the protocol number is everything. Without it, your email joins a generic inbox. With it, the recipient can find your file immediately.

**Structure:**
- **Subject line:** "Seguimento: [Protocol number] / [Your name]"
- **Body:** Reference the original submission date. State what you submitted. Ask for a status update or estimated timeline.
- **Key line:** "Gostaria de saber o estado atual do processo." (I would like to know the current status of the process.)

This works because it makes the official's job easy. They can search the protocol number, check the status, and respond in 2 minutes.

### Format 3: The Pre-Meeting Request

Before visiting a government office in person, send an email requesting a meeting or clarifying what documents you need to bring. This prevents the most common waste of time: showing up without the right paperwork.

**Structure:**
- **Subject line:** "Pedido de agendamento / [Topic]"
- **Body:** State what you need to discuss. Ask what documents are required. Propose 2 to 3 possible dates.
- **Key line:** "Poderia indicar os documentos necessários para esta reunião?" (Could you indicate the required documents for this meeting?)

This works because Portuguese officials prefer prepared meetings. An email that shows you have done your homework gets a faster response than one that asks them to do the thinking for you.

## What to Apply

4 rules that apply across all 3 formats:

1. **Always write in Portuguese.** Even if the official speaks English. Portuguese emails get processed in the Portuguese language queue. Use a translator if needed.
2. **One request per email.** Multiple questions in one email create processing friction. Each question should be a separate email with a clear subject line.
3. **Include your NIF in every email.** The **NIF** (Número de Identificação Fiscal) is your identity in the Portuguese government system. Without it, you are nobody.
4. **Follow up after 10 business days.** Not 3 days. Not 5 days. 10 business days is the standard processing expectation. Following up earlier signals impatience; waiting longer risks your request being buried.
