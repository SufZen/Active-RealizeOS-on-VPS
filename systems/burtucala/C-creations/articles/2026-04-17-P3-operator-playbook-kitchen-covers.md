# How a 10sqm Kitchen Change Added 30% More Covers

**Category:** Entrepreneurship / Real Estate | **Pillar:** Space, Design & Brand

## The Challenge

A restaurant operator in greater Lisbon was averaging 45 covers per lunch service in a venue designed for 60. The dining room had the space. The team had the skills. The demand was there, especially on weekdays when the surrounding office buildings emptied for lunch.

The bottleneck was invisible from the dining room. It was in the kitchen.

## The Principle

In F&B operations, the kitchen determines output. Not the dining room. When you design from the front of house backward (how many seats can we fit?), you optimize for aesthetics. When you design from the back of house forward (how many plates can the kitchen produce per hour?), you optimize for revenue.

This is the **Operations-Backward Design Principle.** Start with throughput, then design the customer-facing space to match.

## The Steps

### Step 1: Identify the actual bottleneck

The original kitchen was 22sqm with a classic L-shaped layout. 2 prep stations, 1 cooking line, 1 pass area, and a dishwashing zone. The layout required the chef to cross the dish zone to reach cold storage, adding 8 to 10 steps per plate during service.

Production capacity: 50 plates per service. The dining room seated 60. The gap between kitchen capacity and seating capacity was 10 covers per service, or approximately 16%.

### Step 2: Redesign for flow, not size

The solution was not a bigger kitchen. It was a better-organized one. 3 changes:

1. **Moved cold storage adjacent to the prep station.** Eliminated the crossing pattern. Saved 8 to 10 steps per plate.
2. **Converted the underused pantry (4sqm) into a dedicated dessert and cold prep zone.** This freed the main cooking line for hot production only.
3. **Relocated the dishwashing entry point** from the middle of the kitchen to a pass-through window at the edge. Dirty dishes no longer crossed the clean plating area.

Total space changed: 10sqm was reallocated. No walls moved. No permits required. The kitchen footprint stayed at 22sqm.

### Step 3: Verify compliance

Every kitchen change in Portugal must comply with **HACCP** (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points) regulations. The redesigned flow actually improved HACCP compliance because the clean and dirty circuits no longer crossed. The municipal health inspector approved the changes in a routine inspection.

Important: any F&B layout change should be reviewed against HACCP requirements before implementation. Moving a refrigerator seems simple, but if it creates a temperature break in the cold chain or crosses a dirty circuit, it violates food safety regulations.

### Step 4: Test and measure

After the changes, the kitchen ran at 65 plates per service. The dining room, which seated 60, was now the constraint. But the operator did not need to add seats. They added a second lunch sitting with 30-minute overlap, increasing total covers from 45 to 58 per service. A 29% increase.

## The Outcome

10sqm reallocated. Zero additional construction cost (existing equipment was moved, not replaced). HACCP compliance maintained. Revenue increase of approximately 30% on lunch service.

The change took 3 days of kitchen downtime. Cost: electrician and plumber for utility reconnections, approximately 1,200.
