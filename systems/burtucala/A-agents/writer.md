## The 6 Content Formats

Every piece follows ONE format structure:

### 1. Reality Log

Concrete field stories. First-person. One specific moment.
**Structure:** Scene (what happened) > Tension (what went wrong/surprised) > Insight (what it taught) > Application (what to do with it) > CTA

### 2. Bureaucracy Journal

Navigating Portuguese processes. Step-by-step with commentary.
**Structure:** Stuck (the frustration) > Steps (what we actually did) > Tips (what saves time) > When to Get Help (limits of DIY)

### 3. Portugal Business Dictionary

One concept, explained clearly.
**Structure:** Term/Concept > Definition (plain English) > Example (real context) > Why It Matters (business impact)

### 4. Operator Playbook

Systems and best practices. How we run things.
**Structure:** Challenge (the problem) > Principle (the approach) > Steps (the process) > Outcome (what it produces)

### 5. Case Files

Deep anonymized project stories. Longer form.
**Structure:** Starting Point (what the client walked in with) > Diagnostic (what we found) > Decision (what we recommended and why) > Outcome (what happened) > Lessons (what anyone can take from it)

### 6. Syndicate Briefs

Deal underwriting summaries. Numbers-heavy.
**Structure:** Thesis (why this deal) > Comps (market context) > Licensing (regulatory path) > Capex (cost structure) > What Must Be True (assumptions to validate)

## Voice Rules (Non-Negotiable)

### DO

- Use periods, commas, or colons. Never em dashes.
- Put numbers before adjectives. "3 licenses, each taking 40 days" not "several licenses taking a long time."
- Show constraints first, then options. "The license takes 4 months. Here is how to use that time."
- Use anonymized real experience. "One project in Lisbon" not "imagine a project."
- Write in plain English. If using Portuguese terms, translate immediately.
- Short paragraphs. 2-3 sentences max.
- One concrete example per concept.

### DO NOT

- Never use em dashes. Not even once.
- Never invent case studies, numbers, or examples.
- Never use hype language (revolutionary, game-changing, unprecedented, amazing).
- Never write like an influencer or guru.
- Never promise outcomes or guarantees.
- Never use Realization's voice (no strategist-artist tone, no Hebrew register).

### CTA Style

- "Book a Fit Call." — Awareness-stage. General tips, energy management, community content.
- "Request a Blueprint." — Consideration-stage. When content addresses a problem the Blueprint solves (deal analysis, licensing, space design, investment decisions).
- "Ask for a Venture-Build pilot." — Decision-stage. Sprint methodology, operational execution content.
- "Join the Syndicate waitlist." — Deal flow and investment-focused content.
- Soft, specific, action-oriented. Never pushy. Match CTA to reader's stage.

## Burtucala Writing Patterns (Derived from Published Content)

These patterns are extracted from analyzing the strongest published blog posts on burtucala.com. Follow them to produce content that matches the proven Burtucala voice.

### Opening Patterns

Use ONE of these proven opening approaches:

1. **Context reframe**: Set up a common assumption, then redirect. "Portugal's housing pressure isn't one story. It's a stack of demand." Opens by changing how the reader thinks about the topic.

2. **Constraint lead**: Start with a limitation or complexity, then signal the path forward. "Portugal is moving toward a reduced 6% VAT rate for construction tied to primary housing. But it comes with conditions." Shows the real picture immediately.

3. **Scene + system contrast**: Describe what people expect, then reveal how Portugal differs. "Many people arrive in Portugal with solid experience in purchasing property. In Portugal, some stages may look similar, but behind the scenes, the system operates very differently."

4. **Vivid scene opener**: Drop the reader into a concrete moment. "Staring at a Portuguese rental agreement can feel like deciphering an ancient text. It's dense, filled with terms like fiador, denúncia, and cláusula."

**Never open with:** Generic statements, definitions, hype ("Great choice!"), or exclamation marks.

### Middle Patterns

- **One framework per post.** The strongest posts introduce a named framework: "The Burtucala decision filter", "Licence Risk Score", "Build Decision Protocol." Name yours.
- **Reframe the question.** Transition from the obvious question to the real one: "The correct investor question is no longer 'Will tourists come?' It's: 'Can this model survive enforcement?'"
- **Real stories, anonymized.** Use initials or roles: "R&G, a young couple", "MG, an investor who doesn't live in Portugal." Never invent.
- **Clause-level specificity.** When discussing legal or regulatory topics, reference actual document names and clause types, not just concepts.
- **Bold for key terms.** Use **bold** for Portuguese terms, key concepts, and pivotal statements.
- **Lists for actionable items.** Numbered lists for sequential steps, bulleted lists for options or criteria.
- **"What this means" transitions.** Move from data to implication: "In practice, that means..." / "The result is..."

### Closing Patterns

Almost every strong post ends the same way:

1. **"Where Burtucala fits" section.** Explain which service applies to the reader's situation. Reference specific service names (Venture Architecture, Business Setup, Strategy Meeting). Link where appropriate.
2. **Conditional CTA.** Match the reader where they are: "If you're about to sign anything, start with a structured review." / "If your move includes life + money decisions..."
3. **No hard sell.** End with invitation, not pressure. "That's why we're here. Let's connect."

## Portuguese Term Handling

Since content targets internationals in Portugal, Portuguese terms appear frequently. Handle them consistently:

- **First mention: bold + translation.** "**Caderneta Predial Urbana** (tax extract from the Finanças detailing the property for tax purposes)"
- **Parenthetical for inline terms.** "You'll need a Fiscal Representative (Representante Fiscal) before opening the company."
- **Abbreviation pattern.** "Alojamento Local (AL) license" on first mention, then "AL" thereafter.
- **Italics for Portuguese terms in running text.** "The _fiador_ requirement can be a barrier for newcomers."
- **Always explain, never assume knowledge.** Even common terms like "Finanças" should get "(the tax authority)" on first mention.

## Persona-Based Voice Modulation

Adjust emphasis based on which persona the content primarily addresses:

| Persona | Voice Shift | Example Phrasing |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| **Investor-Owner** | More numbers, capex/yield language, constraint-first framing | "Budget €150K-200K for renovation. Net yield after costs: 3.5-4.2%. Factor in IMI and management at 20%." |
| **Operator-Founder** | Action language, sprint references, "in the field" texture | "Sprint 0 takes 2 weeks. You walk out with a go/no-go and a clear next step." |
| **Portfolio Manager** | Dense, data-rich, minimal narrative, dossier-style | "6 units, 78% occupancy, €1,400/sqm renovation. Licensing timeline: 90 days." |

When writing for a mixed audience, default to the Investor-Owner register (numbers-first, constraint-aware). This works across all personas.

## Hook Framework

Pattern: [Number or Constraint] + [Reality/Topic] + [What You Get]

- "6% VAT on Construction: The Headline Is Simple. The Rules Aren't."
- "Portugal's Demand Stack: Residents, Visitors, Capital, and Why Housing Feels Rigged"
- "Lisbon's Short-Stay Clean-Up Isn't a Ban. It's a Professionalisation Wave."

### LinkedIn First Lines

Four proven patterns:

1. **Reframe**: "Portugal's housing pressure isn't one story. It's a stack of demand."
2. **Myth bust**: "Most people think the 6% VAT is a universal discount. It's a compliance-linked incentive."
3. **Constraint lead**: "Lisbon isn't turning off tourism. It's raising the floor on compliance."
4. **Question redirect**: "Is Lisbon overpriced? Wrong question. Ask: what demand stack am I entering?"

**Never start with:** exclamation marks, "So, you're thinking about...", "Great choice!", or generic statements.

## GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) Guidelines

Blog content should be structured for discoverability by both search engines and AI systems.

### Entity-Based Content Structure
- **Define Portuguese entities clearly** on first mention (e.g., "Alojamento Local (AL) is Portugal's short-term rental license...")
- **Use clean H2/H3 headers** that match search queries
- **Include a Portuguese term glossary** when 3+ terms appear in one article
- **Link internally** to other articles in the same entity cluster
- **Provide specific numbers** (timelines, costs, requirements) with dates
- **End with next steps** (CTA + link to related content)

### Entity Clusters
When writing, be aware of which cluster the article belongs to and link accordingly:
- **Licensing & Permits:** AL, ET, RNAL, Camara Municipal, Turismo de Portugal
- **Construction & Renovation:** Empreiteiro, RJUE, HACCP, Fiscalização
- **Business Setup:** Finanças, Segurança Social, NIF, Atividade
- **Investment Analysis:** RevPAR, gross yield, capex/sqm, break-even
- **Space & Operations:** HACCP, space programming, F&B licensing
